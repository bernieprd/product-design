# Portfolio Critique — Senior/Lead Product Designer Lens

*Evaluated as a PD hiring manager and TA specialist reviewing a candidate for a Senior or Lead Product Designer role.*

---

## First impression (0–10 seconds)

The visual brand is **confident and distinctive** — dark, typographically bold, minimal. It signals taste and conviction immediately. The tagline "product designer · supply side" is a strong niche claim that differentiates you before a single case study is read. This is a good hook for roles in marketplaces, fintech, or ops-heavy products.

**Risk:** For generalist or consumer-focused PD roles, "supply side" may read as self-limiting. Consider whether that trade-off is intentional.

---

## Strengths — what to keep

1. **Quantified impact on every project.** $5M protected revenue, 4.8% revenue uplift, 4% booking increase — these numbers make the portfolio scannable for hiring managers who spend 2–3 minutes max. Most candidates don't have these. Keep them prominent.

2. **Strategic depth over visual showcase.** Each case study reads like a PM or strategy doc — problem framing, trade-off analysis, phased rollout, measurement. For Lead-level roles, this is exactly the right register.

3. **Clear problem ownership.** The narrative of "I identified, shaped, and delivered" comes through consistently, especially on Star Sitter and Service Areas.

4. **Iterative process is visible.** Showing Phase 1 → 5 on Star Sitter, and the fairness fixes after launch, signals maturity — you understand that design doesn't end at launch.

5. **Visual brand.** The site itself is a design artifact. The typography, spacing, and dark aesthetic demonstrate craft judgment without being showy.

---

## Gaps & risks — what could cost you the role

**1. Zero UI artifacts visible in the case studies.**
The biggest gap. Every case study is paragraphs of text with atmospheric background images — no wireframes, no final screens, no prototypes, no before/after comparisons. A hiring manager has no way to evaluate your visual design quality, interaction design decisions, or design systems thinking. This is table stakes for any portfolio review.

> *Risk level: High.* Many teams will skip past a case study that doesn't show screens. "What does this designer actually make?" is unanswered.

**2. All 4 projects are from one company.**
100% Rover portfolio signals depth but zero breadth. Hiring managers wonder: Can this person onboard fast into a new context? Do they have range? Did they leave Glovo and Stuart because they produced nothing worth showing?

> *Risk level: Medium-High.* Even one case study from a different domain (Stuart, Glovo, or a side project) breaks the single-company perception.

**3. No visual process or research artifacts.**
Research is mentioned throughout ("we ran studies," "we identified three mental models") but nothing is shown — no user quotes, no insight cards, no research synthesis, no decision matrices. A Lead-level candidate needs to show how they think and structure ambiguity, not just what they shipped.

> *Risk level: Medium.* Especially for roles where research rigour or design process leadership is evaluated.

**4. The "in flight" case study is a double-edged sword.**
Graduated Fees is interesting strategically but it has no outcome data and describes work still in testing. Without results, it reads as incomplete. Some managers will credit you for showing strategic work-in-progress; others will penalize you for a case study with no resolution.

> *Suggestion:* Frame it explicitly as a strategy and process showcase, not a results case study. Add a "what we're measuring" section so it feels deliberate, not unfinished.

**5. No evidence of Lead-level behaviours.**
The content is strong on individual contributor signals — designing, deciding, shipping — but thin on:
- Mentoring or growing other designers
- Influencing product strategy upstream (vs. responding to it)
- Managing stakeholder conflict or trade-off negotiations
- Setting design standards, critique practices, or design system decisions

> *Risk level: High for Lead/Staff roles.* If you're applying for a Lead, one paragraph per case study on "how I involved/grew the team" would materially change the read.

**6. Typography wraps awkwardly at the projects page.**
"selected work" breaks as "sel-" / "ected work" across lines. Small thing, but for a designer being evaluated on craft, broken display text is noticed.

**7. No resume or CV link.**
Recruiters and sourcers frequently want to cross-reference timelines, titles, and education. There's no PDF resume accessible from the portfolio — you're making them hunt for it on LinkedIn, which is friction.

**8. No back-navigation between case studies.**
Once inside a case study, the only navigation is "about" and "projects." There's no "next project →" link. Hiring managers who want to read all 4 in sequence have to keep going back to the gallery, which breaks flow.

## Overall verdict

This is a **strong strategic portfolio** that will get you into conversations for Senior roles at product-led companies, especially in marketplaces, supply-side tooling, or incentive systems. The business impact framing is well above average.

For a **Lead or Staff** title, the portfolio currently undersells you — the missing screens, missing team dynamics, and single-company breadth will be obstacles in interview loops that include portfolio reviews. The content exists in your head; it just needs to get onto the page.
