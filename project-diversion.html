Rover · 2022–2023

Rover estimated that more than 20% of potential marketplace revenue was being lost to off-platform bookings — relationships that started on Rover but completed elsewhere.

I led design on two complementary initiatives: a cross-platform phone number blocking system and an in-product Community Guidelines modal. Together, they reduced the ability and incentive to move conversations off Rover, delivering an estimated combined upside of roughly $5M+ in protected annual Rover revenue in the highest-risk segments.

We framed a simple program: use Community Guidelines inside the product to set expectations and tie behaviour to safety; use phone number blocking across app, web, and SMS to enforce those expectations at the moment of action — without harming overall booking rates.

What we shipped

The Community Guidelines modal reached sitters and owners at critical moments: existing users on their next login, new owners when sending their first request, new sitters after completing booking onboarding. Users had to explicitly tap "Got it" to dismiss — if they closed the app instead, it re-appeared on the next trigger.

For phone blocking: on app and web, any personal phone number shared before a booking was intercepted and not delivered. A modal explained why, with a primary action returning the user to edit their message. On SMS, detected numbers were masked and both parties received an automated explanation. Once a booking was confirmed between that pair, the block was lifted.

We added special rules: Rover support numbers are always allowed; Rover relay numbers are blocked from being re-shared, since they're not functional for other users.

How we worked & impact

We deliberately set the bar as "do not hurt booking rates" rather than chasing a headline lift. Across the marketplace, booking rates stayed neutral — we didn't make it harder for people to successfully book.

In the segments that matter most for diversion risk, results were clear: the Community Guidelines modal increased the likelihood that conversations between experienced providers and repeat customers turned into bookings. Phone number blocking increased booking rates specifically in conversations where people tried to share their numbers and were stopped.

When both sides of a conversation had seen the Community Guidelines modal, the odds of a booking were roughly 3.5% higher than control. Phone blocking contributed an estimated annual upside of $3.6M in conversations where numbers were shared. Community Guidelines contributed an estimated $2.2M annually in activated in-app bookings.

The simple story: by setting clearer expectations and enforcing on-platform communication, we kept money and relationships inside the product, at scale, without breaking the booking experience.
