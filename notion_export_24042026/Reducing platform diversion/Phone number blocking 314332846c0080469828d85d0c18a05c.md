# Phone number blocking

## Context and problem

Rover’s marketplace depends on trust and on‑platform bookings. Internal analysis showed that more than 20% of potential take was likely being lost to relationships that started on Rover but completed off platform.

Qualitative research, internal surveys, and message reviews all pointed to the same behaviour: sitters and owners sharing personal contact information, especially phone numbers, before a booking was confirmed. As soon as that happened, conversations typically moved off Rover Messages, which meant:

- Lower visibility into the relationship.
- No Rover Guarantee or support coverage.
- Lost marketplace revenue.

At the same time, there were legitimate reasons to share numbers early, e.g: coordinating meet & greets, handling urgent questions, or reassuring anxious owners. Any solution had to reduce diversion risk without making Rover feel hostile or brittle.

## Goals and success metrics

We set a clear, measurable goal:

Make it harder for sitters and owners to share personal phone numbers before booking, while clearly communicating the value of staying on Rover, and without harming overall booking behaviour or satisfaction.

Primary metric:

- Conversation booking rate (CBR) – our bar was a neutral or better result compared to control.

Secondary metrics:

- Reduction in conversations where a phone number is shared pre‑booking on Rover.
- Neutral impact on 30‑day rebooking rate.
- Neutral impact on CX tickets and social sentiment.
- Clear financial read via an estimated annual return on the feature.

## Role and team

I was the lead product designer for the user‑to‑user phone number blocking experience across app, web, mobile web, and SMS.

I partnered with a product manager, engineers on each platform, data science, CX and Trust & Safety, and legal. My focus was to shape the experience strategy, translate detection and policy constraints into coherent UX, and ensure we learned quickly through experimentation.

## Approach

We treated this as a staged, learning‑driven initiative instead of a one‑shot launch.

First, we shipped a focused MVP: hard‑blocking personal phone numbers in new sitter–owner conversations in the apps until a booking was scheduled through Rover. The detection logic prioritized accuracy over breadth, so that when we blocked a message we were confident it actually contained a number.

We built the experimentation plan into the design work from day one:

- Start with a controlled test to de‑risk impact on CBR and customer sentiment.
- Once results were stable, extend the same behaviour to web, mobile web and SMS, so that users couldn’t simply switch channels to bypass the block.
- In parallel, iterate on the detection algorithm based on real evasive behaviours we observed in the data.

## Solution overview

### App and web

For new sitter–owner relationships, when a user typed a phone number and pressed send:

- The message was intercepted and not delivered.
- A modal explained that personal phone numbers cannot be shared before a booking is confirmed, and why keeping the booking on Rover matters (Rover Guarantee, secure payment, 24/7 support).
- The primary action returned the user to the message composer to edit their text.

![Mobile web experience](Phone%20number%20blocking/image%201.png)

Mobile web experience

Once a booking was confirmed between that owner and sitter, the block was lifted for that specific relationship. Rover support numbers were always allowed. We also blocked sharing of the masked “Rover numbers” that the system assigns per relationship, since they have no utility outside that specific pair and are confusing when re‑shared.

### SMS

![SMS experience](Phone%20number%20blocking/image%202.png)

SMS experience

On SMS we couldn’t block messages at send time, so we designed a masking pattern that preserved the same intent:

- When a personal number was detected in an SMS reply, it was redacted in the conversation and in both users’ SMS views.
- Both parties received an automated system SMS explaining that the number had been masked and why.
- The same system message appeared in the Rover conversation thread.

As with app and web, the block only applied pre‑booking; once the two sides had an accepted booking, numbers were allowed.

## Trade‑offs

1. **Reducing diversion vs. supporting legitimate needs to talk by phone pre‑booking.** 
    
    We solved this by framing the UX around safety and benefits (Rover Guarantee, secure payment, 24/7 support) rather than punishment, and by clearly signalling that calling via Rover relay numbers is still possible in the right contexts.
    
2. **Perfect detection vs. pragmatic coverage.** 
    
    Users quickly tried to evade the block with worded numbers, multi‑line formats, screenshots, and email. We chose to meaningfully raise the effort required to divert without chasing every edge case: extending detection with text‑to‑number translation and adjacent‑number handling, monitoring evasive patterns, and planning targeted improvements (e.g: image/QR detection) where the diversion risk is highest.
    
3. **Cross‑platform consistency vs. technical constraints.** 
    
    App, web, and SMS all behave differently, but users expect one coherent rule set. We aligned everything around a simple contract: before a booking is confirmed, personal and Rover relay numbers are blocked or masked; after booking, they are allowed; Rover support numbers are always allowed.
    

## Impact and metrics

Overall, the experiment achieved what we aimed for: a neutral marketplace impact with clear wins where the feature mattered most.

- Conversation booking rate remained statistically neutral at the all‑market level and above our non‑inferiority threshold, so we did not harm bookings by blocking numbers.

![image.png](Phone%20number%20blocking/image%203.png)

In conversations where a phone number was detected and blocked, booking rates increased significantly versus control, confirming that keeping those relationships on Rover improves conversion.

![image.png](Phone%20number%20blocking/image%204.png)

The share of conversations with detected phone numbers declined in the variant, indicating some genuine behaviour change alongside known evasive tactics.

- For sitters and owners with prior bookings, there was no significant change in bookings or CBR pre‑ vs post‑launch, suggesting we did not drive experienced users away.

Financially, we estimated:

- An all‑up annual impact of around $400k, with a wide confidence interval consistent with a neutral overall effect.
- An annual upside of roughly $3.6M when focusing only on conversations where phone numbers were known to be shared, treated as an upper‑bound view because not all evasive behaviour is captured in that segment.

Taken together, this was enough evidence to recommend global rollout and continued, targeted investment in detection.

## Next steps

Based on the experiment and follow‑up work, the agreed roadmap focused on three areas:

- Global rollout and polish: ship the blocking/masking experience across all major markets and platforms, with localized copy and internal help center support.

- Better coverage of evasive patterns: continue extending detection to multi‑line numbers, mixed digit/word patterns, and high‑risk phrases, and monitor the cost–benefit trade‑off as complexity increases.

- Beyond phone numbers: explore policy and UX options for early email and payment handle sharing pre‑booking, and invest in image tagging for phone‑number screenshots and QR codes where the diversion risk justifies it.

## Learning

The project reinforced a few principles I now carry into other work:

- Protective features work best when they are framed around user value, not just company risk.
- For sensitive systems work, it is often more important to be non‑harmful at the aggregate level and strongly positive in the right segments than to chase an overall headline lift.
- Close collaboration with data science from the very beginning changes the shape of the design work; it forces you to think in terms of non‑inferiority, confidence intervals, and where you actually need precision.
- For behaviour‑driven problems like diversion, the goal is not perfection; it is to meaningfully shift incentives and behaviour while maintaining trust and keeping the system maintainable over time.
