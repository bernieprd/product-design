### Background

Rover currently charges sitters a flat 20% service fee (15% in Europe). Research and social listening suggest that this model does not accurately reflect the actual value Rover provides across the sitter–owner relationship. Most value is delivered upfront, including acquiring clients, providing trust, and offering insurance, yet the fee remains constant even as Rover's role diminishes over time. This mismatch, combined with fee aversion, likely contributes to off-platform diversion.

A previous test lowering fees for repeat bookings (from 15% to 5% in the UK and ES) showed no clear behavioral shift, partly due to limited sample size and lack of segmentation. However, data shows that 20% of sitters generate roughly 75% of Rover's earnings, making them the most relevant group for testing.

Rover now aims to explore a graduated sitter-side fee structure that aligns cost with delivered value. By tying fees to relationship milestones or sitter lifecycle stages, the goal is to make staying on-platform more financially rational for sitters while maintaining revenue stability. This initiative supports the Sitter Experience team's broader objective to increase owner LTV through higher sitter satisfaction and retention.

### Learning Objectives

| Dimension | Specific Research Objective |
| --- | --- |
| **Cognitive load** | Explore how easily sitters understand how the fee structure works, when and why the fee changes, what triggers each milestone, and whether they can mentally map it to their own experience on Rover. Identify points of confusion, incorrect assumptions, or cognitive overload. |
| **Fairness** | Understand whether sitters perceive the proposed fee changes as fair and proportional to Rover's contribution at different stages. Explore what "fairness" means to them and which comparisons (other platforms, their own effort, client value) they use to judge it. |
| **Alignment with Rover's decreasing value** | Assess whether sitters intuitively see a connection between Rover's decreasing involvement over time and the proposed decrease in fees. Determine if this alignment resonates or fosters skepticism. |
| **Likelihood of winning (perceived benefit)** | Explore how sitters perceive the financial consequences of the new model — whether they feel they can "win" (earn more or keep more) under realistic conditions, and if that changes their willingness to stay on-platform. |
| **Agency / Influence** | Understand to what extent sitters feel they can influence their progress within the model — whether milestones feel achievable, controllable, or dependent on owner behavior. |

**Main objective:** Evaluate how sitters understand, interpret, and react to the graduated, relationship-based fee model at a concept level. Focus on clarity, perceived fairness, emotional resonance, and potential points of confusion. This is not about predicting real behaviour, but about identifying whether the concept feels like an improvement, a downgrade, or neutral compared to the current model.

## Part 2 — Discussion Guide

---

**I. Intro**

Hi [Participant's name], I'm [Researcher's name], nice to meet you!

We're here to hear about your experience with Rover and to show you a few early ideas we're exploring. These are just concepts, not real features, so I'd appreciate it if we kept everything we discussed confidential.

You should have received an NDA by email — were you able to sign it? Before we start, is it okay if I record this session? The recording is only for our internal Research team, will be anonymized, and deleted within three months. There are no right or wrong answers today.

At the end, as a token of appreciation, your $60 CAD incentive will be deposited into your Rover Wallet within 4–5 business days.

---

**II. Warm-up / Perceived value of Rover & Motivation**

- How long have you been offering pet care on Rover? What made you start?
- Has your initial motivation changed over time? If yes, how?
- What do you enjoy most about using Rover, and what do you enjoy the least?
- Have you ever wondered whether it's still worth staying active on Rover?

---

**III. Initial Awareness**

- What kind of services do you usually offer on Rover?
- Do most of your bookings tend to be one-time requests, or do you usually work with the same clients on a recurring basis?
- What matters most to you when choosing the kind of clients you work with?
- How do you usually set your prices on Rover? What factors do you consider?
- Imagine we're friends, and I've just started using Rover but I don't understand how payments work. How would you explain it to me in your own words?

> *If the user mentioned fees, continue with IV. If not, jump to V.*

---

**IV. Overall fees fairness**

- When you think about paying a fee on Rover, what kind of feeling does it bring up for you?
- What do you feel you receive in exchange for that fee?
- How would you describe your overall relationship with the fee — mostly accept it, tolerate it, or question it?

---

**V. Agency**

- How do you see Rover fitting into your life? Side job, full-time, or occasional?
- Do you feel that Rover supports you in a way that lets you provide pet care on your own terms? Why or why not?
- What, if anything, do you think should be different?

---

**VI. Initial Reactions & Comprehension** *(after showing abstract concept)*

> "Thanks for sharing. I'm going to share my screen to show you a new concept. This is a mock example, not something currently available on the platform. Please take 1–2 minutes to review how the idea works in theory. After that, I'll ask a few questions about your impressions."

- What's your overall impression of this concept?
- Let's do a quick exercise: imagine I'm a new sitter and you need to explain this concept to me. Just explain it in your own words based on how you understood it — try to include some numbers or percentages as an example.
- Was anything hard to understand? Was there anything that didn't feel completely clear?

---

**VII. Agency & Perceived control**

> "Imagine this new concept goes live tomorrow."

- Is there anything you currently do that you would change because of it?
- Would it affect how you set your prices? If yes, how and why?
- Would it influence which bookings or customers you choose to take? Why?
- Does this concept change how free you feel to decide which bookings to take?

---

**VIII. Distributive fairness**

- Continuing the same exercise: imagine I'm a new sitter and I ask you, "What do I need to do to benefit from this concept?" What advice would you give me?
- Thinking about your own situation, do you think this concept would benefit you?
- Considering the effort you put into your work, do you feel this fee concept gives you something that matches that effort?

---

**IX. Interactional fairness**

- Do you think this concept would work the same for all sitters?
- Who do you think would benefit the most? And who the least?

---

**X. Procedural fairness**

- Based on this example, when do you think the fee change takes effect?
- How easy or hard was it to understand the basic rules of how this concept works?

---

**XI. Motivation**

- How, if at all, would this concept affect your motivation to keep offering services on Rover?
- Would this concept change any of your goals for your work on the platform?

---

**→ Transition to realistic concept (with specific numbers — 47% start, milestones at $250 and $999)**

> "Thanks for sharing your thoughts. Now I'll show you a more realistic version of the same concept, this time with some made-up numbers. They're just meant to illustrate how it could work in practice."

Follow-up questions cover the same dimensions (Comprehension, Agency, Distributive/Interactional/Procedural fairness, Transparency, Motivation) applied to the numbers-based version, plus:

- When you look at these examples, do the goals or levels feel reachable for you?
- Is there anything about how the fee or reward changes that you'd want explained in a more direct way?
- With the number of bookings you usually get, would this concept feel realistic for you?

---

**Closure**

- Beyond this concept, what could Rover do to better support the way you offer pet care?
- Is there anything else you'd like Rover to explore regarding fees in the future?
- If you could change one thing about how Rover handles fees or rewards, what would it be?

## Part 3 — Report: Findings

**URL:** https://roverdotcom.atlassian.net/wiki/spaces/DSN/pages/5468454992  
**Author:** Lizeth Herrera | **Last modified:** Jan 07, 2026  
**Sample:** 28 sitters across 4 segments | **Market:** Canada

---

### Executive Summary

- The concept of a graduated fee model resonates strongly with sitters. Across segments, the principle of "higher fees at the start, lower fees as relationships deepen" is widely understood and viewed as fair in theory, especially compared to the current flat 20% fee. Sitters see the logic of rewarding loyalty and long-term relationships.
- However, the model's **mechanics and structure** create significant friction _independent of the specific starting fee_. Sitters struggled to understand how the model works across services, how milestones are triggered, and whether progress depends on factors within their control. These uncertainties undermine confidence in the model even before numerical examples are introduced.
- A central issue is **structural inequality across service types**. Earnings-based milestones allow boarding providers to reach rewards rapidly, while dog walkers and drop-in sitters require many more bookings to achieve the same result. This creates a perception that the model advantages high-ticket services and makes progress feel unattainable for low-ticket providers.
- Once specific numbers were shown, especially a starting fee near 47%, these underlying concerns intensified. The high initial fee amplifies risk perceptions, causing sitters to become more selective with new clients, avoid one-off or low-value bookings, and adopt defensive pricing strategies.
- Across segments, sitters expressed a sense of **limited agency**. Progress depends heavily on pet parent behavior (repeat frequency, trip patterns), not sitter effort or service quality. This disconnect weakens motivation and makes the system feel unpredictable.
- Sitters expect **clear, simple transparency** for the model to be credible — explicit explanations of how milestones apply across services, how edge cases are handled, and real-time tracking of progress.

---

### Overarching Findings (per construct)

#### Before introducing the new concept

**Awareness of current service fees**
- Sitters are broadly aware that Rover charges a service fee, but this awareness is inferred from payouts rather than grounded in an explicit understanding. Most describe it loosely as "around 15–20%," often with visible uncertainty.
- Awareness is functional, not conceptual. Sitters know a fee exists and accept it as part of platform use, but few can clearly explain how it works.

**Perception of current service fees**
- The current fee is intellectually accepted but emotionally unresolved. Sitters justify it as "the cost of using the platform."
- Perceived legitimacy relies on inferred value (insurance, payments, trust, admin) rather than concrete knowledge.
- For New and Growing sitters, especially those on low-value bookings (e.g., dog walking), the fee feels heavier because it compounds early-stage vulnerabilities (low prices, few reviews, thin margins).

---

#### After introducing the new concept

**Comprehension**
- Across segments, sitters understood the _intent_: repeat relationships are rewarded with lower fees overall.
- Sitters struggle to understand the _mechanics_. Confusion consistently emerges around cumulative value, milestone triggers, permanence of fee reductions, and scope (per client, per service, per sitter).
- Introducing real numbers improves comprehension but worsens evaluation — the 47–50% starting fee triggers strong adverse emotional reactions and reframes the model as punitive.

**Distributive fairness: Am I getting what I deserve?**
- Fairness depends on how quickly effort converts into lower fees. When milestones feel reachable and proportional, fairness holds; when progress is slow or dependent on client behavior, fairness breaks down.
- Service type strongly shapes fairness perceptions. The same milestone can require vastly different levels of effort depending on booking value (a week-long boarding stay vs. many dog walks). This creates structural unfairness rooted in the model's design, rather than individual sitter performance.
- The 47% starting fee is widely perceived as disproportionate to Rover's contribution at the beginning of the relationship, particularly for one-off or low-value bookings.

**Interactional fairness: Am I treated with respect, like everyone else?**
- Although rules are equal, sitters do not perceive equal opportunity. The model is experienced as favoring those already advantaged by service type or pricing power.
- New sitters feel particularly penalized by the high starting fee at the exact moment they need flexibility to compete.
- Established sitters with diversion signals feel overlooked — the model rewards only high-volume, frequent-repeat sitters, undervaluing other forms of contribution (long-term trust, selective availability, high-responsibility care).

**Procedural fairness: Are the rules clear and applied consistently?**
- Procedural confidence weakens once sitters project the model onto real-world scenarios. Edge cases introduce doubt: how are existing repeat clients treated at launch? Do long gaps reset progress? How do milestones apply across mixed service types?
- These unresolved scenarios lead sitters to question not the logic of the system, but Rover's ability to apply it fairly and consistently in practice.

**Transparency**
- Sitters think in terms of what they already know. When a 47% starting fee is presented without explicitly linking it to the existing fee, it feels like a sudden jump. Sitters continue to anchor their expectations to the 20% baseline, making the new model harder to understand.

**Perceived value of Rover**
- Sitters believe Rover provides most of its value at the beginning of a relationship (discovery, trust infrastructure, payments, insurance).
- They understand why Rover would take more at the start and less later. However, when the initial fee is very high (close to 50%), sitters feel it does not match the value Rover is actually providing at that stage.
- If Rover takes a large share of early earnings, sitters expect visible added benefits (better search placement, more profile visibility, promotions). Without these, high fees feel like margin protection rather than value.

**Motivation**
- The model only becomes motivating after milestones are reached. High upfront fees make sitters hesitant to take new clients, experiment with pricing, or accept one-off bookings.
- Only sitters with frequent repeat bookings feel more motivated overall. For everyone else — especially new and growing sitters — the model feels less motivating than the current flat 20% fee.

**Agency & Perceived control**
- Sitters perceive a disconnect between their effort and the reward. Progress relies heavily on factors outside the sitter's control — whether a pet parent decides to rebook, how frequently they travel, the nature of their care needs.
- Sitters offering frequent recurring services feel more able to "work the system." In contrast, sitters with irregular demand, seasonal bookings, or one-off services feel that hard work doesn't guarantee progress.
- The high starting fee makes early-stage decisions feel financially dangerous. Taking on a new client or experimenting with pricing is risky because there's no certainty the client will return.

**Pricing & Client selection behavior**
- The high starting fee completely disrupts the early-stage growth strategy for new sitters (copying local prices, setting lower rates to get first reviews).
- Across all segments, sitters want to become more selective with new clients when early bookings carry high financial risk:
  - **New sitters** normally accept almost any booking for reviews and momentum. With a high starting fee, they shift to avoiding one-offs and low-value jobs.
  - **Growing sitters** prioritize only clients who look likely to return, filtering more aggressively to avoid "wasted" first bookings that never reach a milestone.
  - **Established sitters** intensify their existing client curation and would rely even more on long-term clients.
  - **Established sitters with diversion signals** would avoid most early-phase bookings altogether and focus strictly on known off-platform or semi-off-platform relationships.
- The result: when the first booking becomes the most expensive moment in a relationship, sitters stop taking chances on new or one-time clients.

---

### Recommendations

**Model structure**
- The current earnings-based structure favors high-ticket services (boarding) over low-ticket ones (dog walking). Milestones could be tailored to service type — using _booking counts_ rather than $ values for walkers, or including relationship duration. This ensures effort, not service type, determines access to lower fees.
- Include a clear stance on how existing repeat clients are treated at launch (e.g., partial credit, symbolic recognition, or clear non-retroactivity with justification). Ignoring past effort is perceived as unfair.
- If higher fees are unavoidable, pair them with visible value (e.g., a temporary search boost, a visibility badge, or a faster payout). Without this, high fees signal margin protection rather than partnership.

**UX**
- Display real-time, client-specific progress bars (e.g., "Earn $40 more to unlock 15% fees") to eliminate manual tracking and make the fee structure feel like transparent math rather than a "black box."
- Explicitly state that core protections (insurance, payments, support) remain active at every fee level — reassuring sitters that "lower fees" do not imply "lower safety."
- Label the higher initial fee as covering "Acquisition & Discovery" to clarify why costs start high and decrease as the relationship matures.
- For first-time bookings, shift focus from the high fee to the relationship's potential. Surface data that signals lifetime value (e.g., the pet parent's rebooking rate or platform history) so sitters view the initial higher fee as a calculated investment, not a sunk cost.

**Communications**
- Explain _why_ the fees change before diving into the math. Sitters are more accepting of complex rules when they understand the core logic.
- Acknowledge upfront that the model is detailed and that it's normal not to grasp everything immediately. Make it clear that sitters will learn through simple in-app examples and guided touchpoints.
- Use real-world examples across multiple service types (not just boarding), rotating so the concept doesn't appear biased toward high-ticket services.
- Address complex scenarios early: existing repeat clients, long gaps between bookings, mixed service types. Sitters need to see that there are no hidden rules.
- Emphasize shared benefits of staying on Rover (insurance, secure payments) rather than rules or compliance. Sitters respond better to a partnership approach.
- Phase the communication: start with the "big picture" intent, then explain mechanics, then introduce personalized features. Step-by-step approach prevents overwhelm.
- Be explicit about what pet parents see. Some sitters worried that owners might see their fee levels or milestone progress — clearly stating that fee percentages and sitter incentives are visible only to sitters removes this concern.
