## Part 1 — Study Plan

### Background / Context

We are planning to launch a relationship-based, graduated sitter fee model in Canada. This model is designed to better align platform value with how sitter–client relationships evolve over time, by incentivizing sitters to grow and maintain repeat relationships on Rover and rewarding those relationships with higher take-home earnings.

Adapting the fee structure is a necessary, though not sufficient, step toward increasing take per relationship. For the model to succeed, sitters must clearly understand how fees change and how their actions affect earnings.

In a recent concept test, we found that while sitters are generally motivated by the idea of this model, there is friction around understanding the underlying math and fee progression. This highlights that UX and design clarity are critical enablers of adoption. As a result, validating the usability and design of the fee model is imperative to ensure sitters can navigate it confidently and make informed decisions.

### Research Purpose

Validate whether the UX and communication supporting the new relationship-based fee model enable sitters to understand and use it correctly in practice.

Specifically: can both new and existing sitters discover fee-related information at the right moments, understand how fees change across a client relationship, and confidently complete key tasks (reviewing earnings, setting rates, evaluating booking requests) without confusion or misinterpretation?

---

### Learning Objectives

#### Behavioral / Task-Based

| Objective | Construct | Journey stage | Success | Failure |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Initial awareness** | Awareness | First exposure | Sitters recognize a new fee model exists; understand fees vary by relationship | Sitters don't notice fee touchpoints; believe fee model is unchanged |
| **High-level comprehension** | Conceptual comprehension | Pre-booking | Sitters can explain the basic logic in their own words; understand repeat bookings lead to lower fees | Sitters misinterpret which fee applies; rely on prior 20% mental model |
| **In-context comprehension** | In-context comprehension | Booking evaluation | Sitters correctly identify which fee applies for new vs. existing clients | Sitters misidentify the applicable fee |
| **Discoverability of fee info** | Information discoverability | Booking evaluation | Sitters find fee information without guidance | Sitters struggle to locate fee information; need moderator guidance |
| **Discoverability of Relationship page** | Navigation & page discovery | Booking / relationship mgmt | Sitters navigate to the Relationships page independently; recognize it as source of truth | Sitters don't find or use the Relationships page; misunderstand its purpose |
| **Understanding of tier progress** | System comprehension | Relationship tracking | Sitters correctly explain current progress and next milestone; understand what triggers a fee change | Sitters misunderstand progress indicators; unclear on what triggers changes |

#### Attitudinal

| Objective | Construct | Journey stage | Success | Failure |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Expectation management** | Expectation alignment | Booking / Relationship tracking | Sitters understand when fee changes apply; don't expect retroactive adjustments | Sitters expect money back for past bookings; assume immediate or global fee changes |
| **Cognitive load** | Cognitive load | Booking decision | Sitters process fee information quickly; fees don't dominate decision-making | Sitters spend disproportionate time on fees; express confusion or overload |
| **Decision confidence** | Decision confidence | Booking decision | Sitters express confidence; can decide without external validation | Sitters hesitate due to fee uncertainty; say they'd contact support |

### Hypotheses

1. Even if sitters are aware of the new fee model, many will initially form an incomplete or incorrect understanding of how fees apply in practice.
2. Sitters' understanding significantly increases when fee information is presented in the context of a specific booking, compared to abstract explanations.
3. When discoverable, the Relationships page will serve as a cognitive anchor — a source of truth that reduces confusion about progress and applicable fees.
4. Visual progress indicators (progress bars) may lead some sitters to expect fee changes or retroactive benefits earlier than intended if timing and rules are not explicitly stated.
5. Evaluating new-client bookings introduces higher cognitive load than existing-client bookings due to uncertainty about future progress and fee recovery.
6. Established sitters will show higher confidence and faster decision-making than new sitters, even when both reach the same correct conclusion.
7. Friction increases when sitters perceive that progress toward lower fees depends more on pet parent behavior than on their own actions.
8. Some sitters will correctly understand the model but still feel insufficiently confident to act without additional reassurance.

### Intro Questions

- Tell me about your experience with Rover as a pet sitter.
- How long have you been providing pet care through the platform?
- What types of services do you currently offer?
- What's your client mix like? Mostly one-time, mostly repeat, or a mix?
- How do you usually set your prices?
- *(Pushing awareness)* Is anything deducted or adjusted before you receive your payout?

### Task 0 — Explore Home

You're checking the app to see how things are going with your recent clients. Take a moment to look around and tell me if there's anything new, interesting, or worth checking.

- What caught your attention first? Why?
- What do you think that small notification dot meant? Why didn't you/did you tap it?

---

### Task 1 — Review and start the service

Find Koni's upcoming dog-walking schedule. Tell me what days/times are booked and anything you'd want to double-check before the walk. Then show me how you would start a Rover Card for Koni.

- What are you looking for right now?
- What do you expect to find on this screen?
- Does anything here feel new or different from what you remember?

---

### Task 2 — Check Inbox

Please open your Inbox and check for any new messages or booking requests. Scan what's there and tell me what catches your attention.

- What did you notice first when you opened the Inbox?
- Is anything unclear or missing?
- Does anything feel new or different?

---

### Task 3 — Modify booking

Please continue with this booking request. The pet parent has asked to modify the dates because they made a mistake. Go ahead and update the booking to reflect the new dates.

- Based on what you see, how much do you think you would earn from this booking?
- What information are you using to figure that out?
- What do you think will happen to your fees or earnings if this client keeps booking you?

---

**Check-in** *(pause before Task 4)*

Just thinking about what you've seen so far — without tapping anywhere:
- What's your current understanding of how Rover charges fees?
- Did anything feel new or different from what you expected?
- If you were explaining this new fee system to another sitter, what would you tell them?

---

### Task 4 — Find the Relationship page

Natalie is a new customer. You've already had a couple of interactions with her, and you want to understand how this relationship is going and what it means for your earnings.

*If found:*
- How easy or hard was it to find?
- Does this feel like the right place for this kind of information?

*If not found:*
- Where would you expect this to live in the app?
- What kind of information would you expect to see there?

---

### Task 5 — Understand the Relationship page

Please take a minute to look at this page. Tell me what you think each part means and anything that feels unclear or confusing.

- How would you explain this page to another sitter?
- Based on this page, how do fees work for this relationship?
- What does a "tier" mean to you in this example?
- When do you think the fee actually changes?

---

### Task 6 — Recurring client (Janelle)

Go back to the Inbox. Find your conversation with Janelle. Take a minute to review the conversation and let me know whether she feels like a new or recurring client, and whether you have any upcoming bookings with her.

- How much do you think you're going to earn from this booking?
- If you wanted to check the status of your relationship with her, where would you go?

---

### Task 7 — Service settings

Now that you've seen how relationships and earnings work, imagine you'd like to earn a bit more from house sitting. What would you do in the app?

- Is anything here surprising or unexpected?
- Would you be able to explain from here how the fees work?

---

### Final Wrap-up

- Based on everything you saw in the prototype, how would you describe the way Rover handles fees under this new model?
- How confident do you feel that you understand how this would work?
- If there's one thing Rover could do to make this clearer or easier to understand, what would you suggest?

## Part 4 — Findings Report

**Source:** Figma Slides — shared in #proj-alt-monetization-test on Jan 30, 2026  
**Author:** Lizeth Herrera | **Sample:** 4 Prospect Sitters + 7 Current Sitters

---

### Our Biggest Conclusion

This model is conceptually well-received once understood — with some friction on some sitters for the Tier 1 that increases the initial fee, but after that is okay which is a big win.

However, in its current state, it is too inference-heavy, too abstract at first exposure, and too reliant on motivation over clarity. **The risk is not rejection. The risk is misunderstanding, false confidence, and uneven adoption.**

---

### Key Takeaways

*Legend: [C] = Current Sitters, [P] = Prospect Sitters*

**[C]** Current sitters consistently **noticed** content, tiers, progress indicators, and banners, but simply noticing them was **not enough to understand the model**. Most sitters needed to move across several screens before they could clearly explain how it works.

**[C]** **Understanding did not happen at first contact.** It emerged gradually after repeated exposure and comparison across different contexts, and early interpretations were often wrong or incomplete. This shows the system is learnable, but not intuitive.

**[P+C]** Comprehension improved significantly when current sitters interacted **with existing recurring relationships, where earnings differences were concrete.** In contrast, **new relationships felt abstract and harder to reason about because there was no immediate financial impact.** For prospective sitters, understanding came more easily through the landing page, since they were already in a "learning" mindset and the structured explanation created a clear anchor early on.

**[C]** The experience is weakest at the beginning of a **new relationship.** At that stage, sitters are focused on evaluating the pet and the owner, not on long-term earnings, which **makes progress and tier messaging feel poorly timed and mentally demanding.**

**[P+C]** Visual elements such as colors, progress bars, and tier labels led sitters to infer meaning on their own. This often created **false confidence,** where sitters felt they understood the system but were missing key mechanics.

**[C]** **Inbox was treated as task-focused space.** Sitters showed low willingness to engage with educational content unless it directly explained how the current booking affected their earnings.

**[P+C]** **The landing page was effective at clarifying the model and increasing confidence, but only after sitters made a deliberate effort to seek that information,** rather than learning it naturally through the product.

**[C]** The concept of a 'relationship' is foundational to the model but not immediately intuitive to sitters, who naturally think in terms of bookings.

**[P+C]** Once the model was fully understood, the majority of sitters generally perceived it as motivating and aligned with staying on the platform — especially from tier 2 onward. **This suggests the main challenge is not necessarily acceptance or trust, but clarity, timing, and how the model is introduced.**

---

### Recommendations (Food for Thought)

**Anchor understanding in concrete earnings, not progress mechanics.** Lead with "what you earn now" and "what you earn next" in every in-product touchpoint. Progress bars and tiers should be secondary and supportive, not the primary explanation.
> Example: "You currently earn 89% with Janelle. After $230 more in completed bookings, you'll earn 93%."

**Design new-relationship states to reduce abstraction and effort.** New relationships are cognitively expensive because there is no visible payoff yet. Make the first state explicitly transitional and low-effort to understand.
> Example: Replace "New relationship... starts your progress" with "First booking with Natalie. Earnings increase after $350 in completed bookings."

**Remove ambiguity between 'earnings' and 'bookings'.** The word "progress" consistently triggers the wrong mental model (number of bookings, rewards system). Always specify what is accumulating and why.
> Example: "$110 in booking value counted toward your next earnings tier" instead of "$110 progress."

**Make tiers informational, not interactive-looking.** The tier cards visually read as buttons, creating false affordances and exploratory friction. Redesign them as labeled states or milestones, not selectable elements.
> Example: Horizontal timeline with labeled checkpoints: Tier 1 (70%), Tier 2 (89%), Tier 3 (93%).

**Unify the 'See progress' and 'Relationship' experiences to avoid redundancy.** Users perceived the progress page as repetitive and unnecessary. The system should feel like a single, coherent explanation at different depths.
> Example: One relationship page with a collapsed default view and an expanded "How this affects earnings" section.

**Let emails and the landing page do the heavy lifting for first-time learning.** In-product touchpoints should reinforce, not re-teach, what has already been explained externally.
> Example: In-app copy references: "Learned about tiers in email? Here's how this booking applies."

**Use color with explicit meaning, not implied semantics.** Light vs. dark green requires inference and creates false confidence. Color should be paired with labels that explain state and timing.
> Example: "Pending (after service completes)" vs. "Completed (counts toward tier)".

---

### Before the Roll Out

**Make the earning logic explicit at first exposure.** Right now, users can eventually explain the model, but only after navigating multiple screens and inferring rules. The system must state plainly, in the UI, that progress is based on earnings within the same client relationship — not number of bookings, not rewards, not global totals.

**Eliminate ambiguity between "progress," "earnings," and "bookings".** The word "progress" consistently triggers the wrong mental model. The product must stop using abstract language where precision is required.

**Redesign the new-relationship state (this is the weakest point).** Acknowledge that nothing has been earned yet. Minimize motivational language and focus on what will happen after the first completed booking, not on long-term tiers.

**Reduce redundancy and consolidate "See progress" and "See relationship".** Explicitly name what is accumulating and how it affects payout, rather than relying on implied meaning.

**Make visual encoding unambiguous (color ≠ meaning).** Colors must be labeled or paired with text. Tier cards must not look like buttons unless they are actionable.

**Anchor on concrete money, not motivation.** Before rollout, the system must: lead with "You currently earn X% with this client"; show "$X more until your earnings increase"; de-emphasize motivational copy that does not change behavior.

**"Relationship" is not a natural mental model for sitters.** They think in terms of Clients, Pets, Bookings, Services. Before rollout, the product must either explicitly define what a relationship means in operational terms, or reframe the language to something closer to how sitters already think.

**Ensure in-product learning does not rely on the landing page.** A sitter who never reads the landing page must still be able to explain how earnings increase, know where they stand, and know what happens next.

### Deep Findings — Per Touchpoint

*Saturation key: HIGH = observed repeatedly across most participants; MID = observed across several with some variation; LOW = observed in only a few sessions.*

Touchpoints tested for Current Sitters: Home/Dashboard · Rates · Conversation · See progress · Relationship page · Booking details

---

#### Touchpoint 1 — Home / Dashboard [Current Sitters]

**Awareness:** HIGH | **Intent to click:** LOW | **Perceived interest:** LOW

> "It feels like an ad. Rover takes a large fee, so I assume the message is just saying that the more clients you get, the more money you make and Rover makes more money too."

The relationship-based fee concept resonates in principle, but its Home screen implementation feels abstract and disconnected from immediate actions or earnings. [HIGH]

Sitters perceived these modules as **tips/tricks or advertisement content** and often skimmed or ignored them during normal workflows. [HIGH]

**Recommendations:** Messages relying on motivational framing were skimmed or ignored. Avoid restating obvious logic ("more bookings = more money"). Lead with numbers, not text — they anchor attention faster for fee-related information.

---

#### Touchpoint 2 — Rates [Prospect Sitters]

**Visibility:** HIGH | **Intent to click:** HIGH | **Perceived interest:** MID

> "I find this system interesting because it rewards people for continuing to work through Rover. The more you use the platform, the more it benefits you in terms of earnings, which feels fair."

For prospect sitters this screen was perceived as fair and transparent. Showing "You'll earn between $42.00 and $55.80" signals that earnings are not fixed, but prospect sitters immediately filled the gap with assumptions. When asked to explain it back, some accurately reconstructed the model: start at Tier 1, move up after earning thresholds, keep more over time. [MID]

---

#### Touchpoint 3 — Inbox [Current Sitters]

**Visibility:** HIGH | **Intent to click:** LOW | **Comprehension:** LOW

> "It does interrupt the flow of the conversation... It would be nicer if maybe it was a banner at the top of the listing or at the very bottom — for me it would definitely distract me from answering them right away."

The conversation carries high cognitive load already. **The inbox functions as a transactional space, not a discovery surface.** Content that doesn't directly support the immediate action is likely to be skipped or perceived as annoying. [HIGH]

**Recommendations:** For new relationships, anchor the message in something concrete and neutral. "See progress" should have lower visual weight so it doesn't compete with primary actions (Book / Modify request).

---

#### Touchpoint 4 — See Progress [Current Sitters]

**Comprehension:** MID

> "I don't really know how this works. I'm assuming the longer my client, the more money I make or something, but I don't see it. It's not showing me that here — how it works."

The visual design clearly signals that something is advancing, but **it does not clearly explain what is progressing** (earnings, bookings, or relationship status), leaving users to infer meaning on their own. [HIGH]

Placing tiers at the top introduces an unfamiliar concept early in the experience. The messaging feels more motivational than practical, **adding cognitive load when users are seeking concrete information.** [MID]

**Recommendations:** Establish the core rule before showing progress visualizations: "The more you work with the same client, the lower Rover's fee becomes." Introduce structure first, numbers second.

---

#### Touchpoint 5 — See Relationship [Current Sitters]

**Comprehension:** MID-LOW

> "I see that the amount they pay is what goes on your relationship status thingy... it shows 165 is how much they have towards your relationship, not the 115 that you receive."

Several participants thought this was the same page they'd already seen — **this feels redundant.** [MID] Users noticed light green vs. dark green states but had to infer what each color represented. [HIGH] For new relationships, users focused first on pets and booking information. [HIGH]

Only after exploration did most users notice the question mark icon. Clicking it produced an "aha moment," but users then struggled with "bookings" vs. "earnings" language. [MID]

**Recommendations:** Make it explicit that this is the relationship overview, not another progress indicator. Surface tier logic unavoidably — not behind a question mark alone. Replace "bookings" with "earnings with this client" throughout.

---

#### Touchpoint 3 (Prospects) — Booking Details [Prospect Sitters]

**Visibility:** MID-HIGH | **Intent to click:** LOW | **Perceived interest:** LOW

"Counts toward your progress" creates the need to click "See progress" — users complete their understanding by interacting with these two touchpoints together. [HIGH] This touchpoint alone increases awareness that progress exists but doesn't explain the model unless the user has already read the landing page. [HIGH]

**Recommendations:** Use this screen for reassurance, not education. Separate earnings clarity from progress messaging. Distinguish between money earned now and progress toward future fee changes.
