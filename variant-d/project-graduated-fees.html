01 · Strategy

The Flat Fee Problem

Today, Rover charges the same fee on a sitter's first booking with a new client as on their 20th booking with a long-term trusted relationship. Frustration about fees is a core driver of diversion.

Internal analysis: capturing repeat-relationship diversion could make Rover's take ~40% higher. The provider strategy targets ~23% take-per-relationship growth. A flat fee doesn't map to that.

What this project is
A strategic, in-flight project to test a model where the fee starts higher on first bookings with a client and decreases as that relationship grows — aligning fees with the value Rover actually provides.

02 · Model Selection

Three Models, One Test

We mapped three possible models: new vs. repeat; relationship-based GBV (fee drops as cumulative booking value with a specific client hits milestones); and account-based GBV (fee drops as total Rover earnings hit milestones).

Relationship-based was the best match to sitter mental models. They think per client, not per year. Fairness, for sitters, is about this relationship — not their total annual earnings.

Frame 01

Model evaluation matrix — 3 models × 5 dimensions (comprehension, fairness, gaming risk, revenue impact, segment impact). Shows the framework used to select relationship-based GBV as the test model.

The evaluation framework was co-created with PM and finance as part of the strategy process.

03 · Design

The Relationship Page

Higher fee on the first slice of relationship GBV (e.g. 28–35%), lower fee once the relationship hits a milestone (e.g. 10–15% after $X). Each owner–sitter pair has its own progress; once unlocked, the lower tier is permanent.

Sitters currently cannot see GBV per client or fees per relationship. A multistep GBV model is complex. I concluded: a dedicated Relationship page is necessary — GBV progress tracker, fee tier, transaction history per client.

Frame 02

MVP Relationship page: GBV progress bar (current value vs. milestone threshold), current fee tier badge, locked next-tier preview, transaction history per client. The core explainability layer.

Makes an abstract model tangible for sitters at every GBV stage.

04 · UX Challenges

Hard Problems

Model mechanics comprehension: sitters struggled with cumulative GBV, milestones, permanent reductions. We leaned on progressive disclosure and anchored everything around "this relationship with this client."

Sense of agency: some milestones depended on owner behaviour, not sitter effort. We focused UI on what sitters control — rebooking on platform, keeping relationships active — and were careful not to over-promise.

Service inequality trade-off
Earnings milestones naturally favour boarding over walking. We accepted this given boarding's revenue share, baked it into messaging, and flagged point-based rebalancing as a future option.

05 · What We're Measuring

Strategy & Process Showcase

As a test-phase project, success isn't a single metric — it's a signal set. We're tracking comprehension and perceived fairness among top sitters, diversion rates on new relationships, repeat booking behaviour over 90-day windows, and per-relationship GBV growth vs. control.

A clean signal on all four → broader rollout.
