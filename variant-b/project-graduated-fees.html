01 · Strategy

The Flat Fee Problem

Today, Rover charges the same fee on a sitter's first booking with a new client as on their 20th booking with a long-term, trusted relationship. That misalignment drives diversion — sitters feel they're paying full platform fees for value they're no longer getting from the platform.

Internal analysis shows that capturing repeat-relationship diversion could make Rover's take ~40% higher. The provider strategy North Star targets ~23% take-per-relationship growth over the next few years. A flat fee doesn't map to that reality.

"Sitters think per client, not per year. Fairness, for them, is about this relationship — not their total annual earnings."

02 · Model Selection

Three Models, One Clear Winner

We mapped three possible models: new vs. repeat; relationship-based GBV (fee drops as cumulative booking value with a specific client hits milestones); and account-based GBV (fee drops as total Rover earnings hit milestones).

Relationship-based was the best match to sitter mental models. New vs. repeat was too easy to game. Account-based was clean but misaligned with how sitters think about fairness — they think per client, not per year.

Test market: Canada
Testing relationship-based GBV first in Canada as a proxy for the US market. Account-based retained as a complementary option for a later phase.
03 · The Relationship Page

A Dedicated Source of Truth

The test model: higher fee on the first slice of relationship GBV (e.g. 28–35%), lower fee once the relationship hits a milestone (e.g. 10–15% after $X). Each owner–sitter pair has its own progress.

Sitters currently can't see GBV per client or fees per relationship. A multistep GBV model is conceptually complex. I explored multiple solutions and concluded: a dedicated Relationship page is necessary — a single source of truth for a sitter–client pair, with a GBV progress tracker, fee tier, and transaction history.

04 · UX Challenges

Hard Problems, Design Solutions

Model comprehension was the central challenge — sitters struggled with cumulative GBV, milestones, and permanent reductions. We leaned on progressive disclosure and anchored messaging around "this relationship with this client."

Sense of agency: some milestones depended on owner behaviour, not sitter effort. We focused UI on what sitters control — rebooking on platform, keeping relationships active — and were careful not to over-promise.

Service inequality trade-off
Earnings milestones naturally favour boarding over walking/drop-ins. We accepted this given boarding's revenue share, baked it into messaging, and flagged point-based rebalancing as a future option.
