06 / MODEL

The 3-tier
relationship model.

Each owner–sitter pair has its own GBV track. Fee starts higher, drops once the relationship hits a milestone. Once unlocked, the lower tier is permanent.

  • Per relationship: each owner–sitter pair tracked independently
  • GBV-based: cumulative booking value, not number of bookings
  • Permanent: milestone unlocked = lower tier forever
  • No recency in v1: no running-in-place pressure

07 / INSIGHT
Sitters think about fees per client,
not per year.

Account-based (annual GBV) is cleaner to implement. But when we talked to sitters, they track clients — not totals. 'I've been with this family for 3 years and I still pay 20%.' Relationship-based maps to how fairness actually feels.

Top sitter earnings share
~75%
Top sitters drive ~75% of Rover's earnings. This model is designed to resonate with them.
08 / DESIGN

Designing a regressive tax that feels transparent.

01 Model Comprehension

Cumulative GBV, milestones, permanent reductions — sitters struggled with all three at once.

02 Perceived Fairness

Boarding ($500/night) reaches milestones faster than walking ($20/walk). Is that fair?

03 Sense of Agency

Milestone progress depends on owner repeat behaviour, not just sitter effort. How do you design for that?

04 Threshold Crossings

A booking that crosses a tier boundary mid-transaction. Split fee vs. cashback — which is right?

CHALLENGE 01

Comprehension.

Sitters need to understand: what GBV is, how it accumulates, when the tier drops, and that it's permanent. That's four mental models at once.

DECISION

Progressive disclosure. Anchor everything on 'this relationship with [client name]', not abstract platform rules. The relationship page is the source of truth — you don't need to understand the model to use it.

CHALLENGE 02

Perceived fairness.

Milestone thresholds based on GBV naturally favour high-ticket services. A boarding sitter reaches $500 in 2–3 bookings. A walker needs 25+.

DECISION

Accepted in v1 given boarding's revenue share and top-sitter focus. Messaging anchors on the relationship, not the math. Longer term: point-based systems can rebalance across services.

CHALLENGE 03

Sense of agency.

Reaching a GBV milestone depends partly on the owner repeating — not just sitter quality or effort. That felt unfair in research.

DECISION

Focus UI and messaging on what sitters control: booking on-platform, keeping relationships active, sending post-stay messages. Never show progress as purely dependent on owner behaviour.

ACTIONS IN YOUR CONTROL

Remind client to rebook via Rover
Send post-stay message to stay top of mind
Waiting for client to decide (not shown)
CHALLENGE 04

Threshold crossings.

When a single booking crosses the GBV milestone mid-transaction, how do you apply the fee? Two options were debated at length.

DECISION

Split-fee is the truthful model for the relationship test. Cashback is easier to display but feels delayed and transactional. Backlogged for when account-based layer is added.

Split-fee CHOSEN

First $X of booking: 28% fee. Remaining amount: 12% fee. Applied in the same transaction. Accurate and honest.

Cashback

Full 28% at booking time. Rebate issued after. Easier to display — but delayed reward feels arbitrary. Backlogged.

09 / KEY ARTIFACT

The Relationship Page.

The central UX artifact that makes the model legible. Without it, sitters have no way to verify their tier, check progress, or understand why a fee changed.

  • GBV progress tracker per client
  • Current fee tier badge
  • Locked next-tier preview
  • Per-client transaction history
  • Rebook CTA — keeps flow on-platform
DESIGN PRINCIPLE

You don't need to understand the model to use it — you just need to see your progress.

12 / NEXT MOVES

Two paths forward.

IF SIGNALS ARE STRONG
  • Tune GBV thresholds based on conjoint data
  • Broader rollout to US markets
  • Layer account-based track as a complementary loyalty signal
  • Explore point-based system to rebalance across service types
IF SIGNALS ARE MIXED
  • Explore point-based adjustments to rebalance across services
  • Consider a simpler account-based layer for overall loyalty
  • Iterate on Relationship page to close comprehension gaps
  • Revisit threshold mechanics based on sitter feedback

Long-term: account-based track as a complementary path — where a status model (like airlines) makes sense for sitters who want yearly loyalty rewards, without undermining the relationship-level mechanic.

REFLECTION
Fee design is experience design.

A model that sitters can understand, track, and trust is as important as one that works financially. The Relationship page isn't a nice-to-have — it's what makes the model real.
