Rover · 2023–2025

Rover already had work in motion around successful sitters: onboarding best practices, booking walkthroughs, and a Sitter Insights dashboard. Despite that, three core gaps remained: owners had a hard time identifying our most reliable sitters in search; sitters saw metrics but didn't feel a strong motivation to change behaviour; and off-platform diversion remained a large, strategic revenue leak.

We built Star Sitter as a third lever — a provider-level status program that gamifies behaviours we care about, directs demand to our most reliable supply, and ties status to on-platform repeat behaviour. In experiments, it increased new-to-Rover revenue per customer by 4.8% and extrapolated to roughly $2.5M in incremental annual revenue.

As lead product designer, I co-led the design from first concept through global rollout and major iterations — including the end-to-end status model, owner and sitter surfaces, and the "explainability" layer in Sitter Insights.

Phase 1–2 · Foundation and beta

We launched the Sitter Insights dashboard first — metrics like response rate, booking rate, calendar updates, and cancellations — establishing the mental model: "this is where I understand how my business is doing." Small but positive improvements in conversation booking rate (+0.6%) confirmed sitters responded to visibility.

With the data foundation in place, we introduced Star Sitter as a beta in limited markets. Key design choices: a provider-level, non-tiered status; qualification based on response rate, cancellation rate, star rating, new client booking rate, and repeat clients; badge surfacing on search cards and profiles; and a "Only show Star Sitters" owner filter.

The owner-side split test showed the badge meaningfully shifted behaviour — more conversations going to Star Sitters, higher conversion, and higher average booking values. At the same time, CX sentiment surfaced early cracks: confusion around booking rate, and pain around edge cases like long-tenured but low-activity sitters.

Phase 3–4 · Understandable and fair

We iterated on the public landing page and the Sitter Insights tracking tab. On the landing page, usability tests showed sitters skimmed past key rules. We tightened copy to use direct, instructive phrasing, pulled core rules into scannable bullets, and added a prominent "Check your progress" CTA sending sitters straight into Sitter Insights.

On tracking, we reshaped the tab: a clearer distinction between past snapshots and current-period metrics; a simple status message ("You are on track to get Star Sitter on {date}"); and tiered "Learn more" patterns for the hardest concepts.

We also made three major fairness changes: moved from lifetime to recent activity (5 recent clients and 2 repeat clients in 6 months); formalised legacying — sitters who met criteria last period but miss one metric can keep the badge for one grace period; and fixed a Contact More Sitters bias that was unfairly penalising high-value sitters' booking rate.

Phase 5 · Rolling status and outcomes

Quarterly refreshes gave us stability at launch, but over time the trade-offs became clear: long delays between behaviour change and status change; predictable CX ticket spikes around refresh days; and a fragile manual batch process. We moved to a rolling model where sitters earn Star Sitter as soon as they meet the criteria, and dropping below criteria puts them into a three-month grace period.

This allowed us to dramatically simplify the Sitter Insights UX: no more qualification period dropdowns, a single real-time picture of where sitters stand, and a status model that better matches sitter mental models.

Across experiments and rollouts: a 4.8% increase in revenue per new-to-Rover customer, driven by higher average booking values and nearly 1 percentage point higher conversion. At full scale, the geo-split extrapolated to roughly $2.5M in incremental Rover revenue. Search behaviour shifted toward sitters with stronger reliability and on-platform habits as more owner conversations flowed to Star Sitters.
