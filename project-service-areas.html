Rover · 2024–2025

On Rover, traveling services mean the sitter goes to the owner's home. Sitters historically set a simple circular radius — easy to build but it ignored real geography and travel patterns. Sitters repeatedly told us: "I'm getting requests that are technically within my radius but practically impossible or financially unreasonable." Owners felt it too — search showed sitters as available, only for them to decline because the trip was too long.

This work started as a Maker Days hackathon: a small team explored whether we could move beyond the "as-the-crow-flies" radius into something that reflected how sitters actually travel. That prototype became the foundation for the eventual product.

As lead product designer, I was responsible for the full travel-distance service area experience — from exploratory research through experiment design, rollout, and the underlying search integration.

Understanding what "distance" means to sitters

We started with a working prototype that translated each sitter's circular radius into a travel-distance polygon using Mapbox's isochrone API — drawing areas based on real roads and barriers instead of a perfect circle. In moderated sessions, sitters reacted to seeing their areas redrawn, adjusted distance vs. time and travel mode, and talked through how far they'd really go for different services.

Three design anchors came out of this: sitters think in both miles and minutes; irregular, road-based shapes feel more intuitive than circles; and they want different areas per traveling service — often wider for house sitting and more local for walks, especially in dense cities.

From there, we defined a single, coherent model. Sitters can define service areas by distance (1–100 km/miles) or estimated travel time (1–60 minutes), with support for walking, cycling, or driving. House sitting, drop-ins, and walks each get their own service area instead of sharing one radius. We backfilled everyone's initial polygon from their existing radius so no one started from scratch.

Solution and impact

We validated in two geographic A/B tests — a smoke test in US, GB, and ES, then a larger rollout covering about 20% of addressable markets. When both waves showed better matching, better conversion, and strong sitter adoption, we committed to global rollout instead of a long 50/50 split.

Results across the two geo tests: for new owners, there was a small initial decrease in conversion (around –1–2% relative) driven by stricter "outside service area" behaviour when locations were imprecise — a few more owners hit "no sitters available here." But for everyone who did see sitters, those sitters were closer and more likely to accept the job.

Search-to-request improved by roughly +2–3% relative and search-to-book by roughly +1.6–2.8% relative. Our internal match-quality score improved by about 7%. The maximum distance of page 1 results dropped by roughly 4–5 miles for walking and drop-ins. New revenue per searcher was positive in both geo sets, with point estimates around +5–6% in US markets.

We traded a small, understandable shift in one early metric for better search quality, shorter owner–sitter distances, and higher search-to-request and search-to-book — in a model that sitters liked and adopted.
