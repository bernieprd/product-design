01 · origin

From Hackathon to Product

Sitters historically set a simple circular radius — easy to build, but it ignored real geography. Sitters repeatedly told us: "I'm getting requests that are technically within my radius but practically impossible." Owners felt it too — search showed sitters as available, only for them to decline because the trip was too far.

This started at Maker Days: a small team explored whether we could move beyond the "as-the-crow-flies" radius into something that reflected how sitters actually travel. That prototype became the foundation for the eventual product.

My role

As lead product designer, I was responsible for the full travel-distance service area experience — from exploratory research through experiment design, rollout, and the underlying search integration.

02 · research

Understanding Distance

We started with a working prototype translating each sitter's circular radius into a travel-distance polygon using Mapbox's isochrone API. In moderated sessions, sitters reacted to seeing their areas redrawn, adjusted distance vs. time and travel mode, and talked through how far they'd go for different services.

Three design anchors emerged: sitters think in both miles and minutes; irregular, road-based shapes feel more intuitive than circles; and they want different areas per traveling service.

Model definition

Sitters define service areas by distance (1–100 km/miles) or estimated travel time (1–60 minutes), with walking, cycling, or driving support. House sitting, drop-ins, and walks each get their own service area. We backfilled everyone's initial polygon from their existing radius so no one started from scratch.

03 · solution & impact

Validated & Shipped

We validated in two geographic A/B tests — a smoke test in US, GB, and ES, then a larger rollout covering ~20% of addressable markets. Both waves showed better matching, better conversion, and strong sitter adoption, so we committed to global rollout.

Search-to-request improved by +2–3% relative, search-to-book by +1.6–2.8% relative. Match quality score improved by ~7%. New revenue per searcher was positive in both geo sets — around +5–6% in US markets.

The conversion trade-off

For new owners, there was a small initial decrease (–1–2% relative) in conversion driven by stricter "outside service area" behaviour. But for everyone who did see sitters, those sitters were closer and more likely to accept. We traded a narrow early metric for better long-run quality.
