01 · overview

The Problem

Rover estimated more than 20% of potential marketplace revenue was being lost to off-platform bookings — relationships that started on Rover but completed elsewhere.

I led design on two complementary initiatives: a cross-platform phone number blocking system and an in-product Community Guidelines modal. Together, they reduced the ability and incentive to move conversations off Rover.

Program framing

Use Community Guidelines inside the product to set expectations and tie behaviour to safety. Use phone number blocking to enforce those expectations at the moment of action — without harming overall booking rates. That was the bar: do not hurt booking rates.

[ wireframe ]

Community Guidelines modal — three trigger states: existing users on next login, new owners on first request, new sitters after booking onboarding. Shows the "Got it" required confirmation and re-appearance logic for dismissed sessions.

02 · phone blocking

What We Shipped

The Community Guidelines modal reached sitters and owners at critical moments. Users had to explicitly tap "Got it" to dismiss — if they closed the app, it reappeared on the next trigger.

For phone blocking: on app and web, any personal phone number shared before a booking was intercepted. A modal explained why. On SMS, numbers were masked and both parties received an automated explanation. Once a booking was confirmed, the block was lifted.

Special rules

Rover support numbers always allowed. Rover relay numbers blocked from being re-shared (not functional for other users). The system had to work across three surfaces: iOS app, web, and SMS — each with different interception capabilities.

[ wireframe ]

Phone blocking flow across surfaces — app interception modal with "Edit message" CTA; web inline warning; SMS automated masking notification. Shows the unified experience across three distinct technical surfaces.

03 · impact

How We Worked & Impact

We deliberately set the bar as "do not hurt booking rates" — not chasing a headline lift. Across the marketplace, booking rates stayed neutral.

When both sides had seen Community Guidelines, the odds of a booking were 3.5% higher than control. Phone blocking contributed an estimated $3.6M annually. Community Guidelines contributed an estimated $2.2M annually in activated in-app bookings.

Segment analysis

The Community Guidelines modal increased the likelihood that conversations between experienced providers and repeat customers turned into bookings. Phone blocking increased booking rates specifically in conversations where numbers were shared and intercepted.
