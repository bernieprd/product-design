01 · strategy

The Flat Fee Problem

Today, Rover takes a flat percentage on every booking — the same fee for a first booking with a brand-new client and for a long-standing, trust-based relationship. Frustration about fees is a core driver of diversion: sitters feel that "paying the same fee forever" no longer matches the value Rover provides.

Internal analysis: if repeat-relationship diversion could be captured, Rover's take could be ~40% higher. The provider strategy North Star is to increase take per relationship by ~23% over the next few years — a flat fee doesn't map to that reality.

What this project is

A strategic, in-flight project to rethink how Rover charges sitters: instead of a flat percentage, a model where the fee starts higher on first bookings with a client and decreases as that relationship grows. Goal: align fees with the value Rover actually provides.

02 · model selection

Three Models, One Test

We mapped three possible models: new vs. repeat (lower fee on all repeat bookings); relationship-based GBV (fee drops as cumulative booking value with a specific client hits milestones); and account-based GBV (fee drops as total Rover earnings hit milestones).

We assessed each along: comprehension, fairness, gaming risk, revenue impact, and segment impact on top sitters who drive ~75% of earnings. We landed on relationship-based — best match to sitter mental models of "fair vs. unfair" fees. They think per client, not per year.

Why not new vs. repeat?

Too easy to game, and weak at directing behaviour. Account-based was conceptually cleaner but less aligned with how sitters actually think about fairness.

03 · the relationship page

A New Source of Truth

The test model: higher fee on the first slice of relationship GBV (e.g. 28–35% up to a threshold), lower fee once the relationship hits that milestone (e.g. 10–15% after $X). Each owner–sitter pair has its own progress; once unlocked, the lower tier is permanent for future bookings.

Core design challenge: sitters currently cannot see GBV per client or how fees are calculated per relationship. I explored small tweaks to payment history and reworking Rebook into a client-centric view — but concluded that a dedicated Relationship page is necessary.

My contributions

Co-creating the evaluation framework, leading the relationship-based experience exploration, writing the deep-dives that informed the 1-pager, and defining the MVP Relationship page scope and touchpoints needed to make the model explainable.

04 · ux challenges

Hard Problems

Model mechanics comprehension: sitters struggled with cumulative GBV, milestones, permanent reductions, and how they applied across services. We leaned on progressive disclosure and anchored everything around "this relationship with this client."

Sense of agency: sitters felt some parts depended heavily on owner behaviour. We focused UI on actions sitters control — rebooking on platform, keeping relationships active — and avoided over-promising on things outside their control.

Perceived inequality across services

Earnings-based milestones naturally favour high-ticket boarding over low-ticket walking. We accepted this trade-off given boarding's revenue share, baked it into messaging, and considered longer-term point systems to rebalance.

05 · what we're measuring

Success Signals

As a test-phase project, success isn't a single metric — it's a signal set. We're tracking:

Comprehension

High comprehension and perceived fairness among top sitters during the Canada test.

Diversion

No material increase in new-relationship diversion despite higher upfront fees.

Repeat Behaviour

Improved repeat booking behaviour on platform for eligible relationships over 90-day windows.

GBV Growth

Per-relationship GBV growth versus control group. A clean signal on all four → broader rollout.
